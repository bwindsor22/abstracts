# Quoridor: Comprehensive Game Research

## Rules Summary

### Components


Quoridor is played on a game board of 81 square spaces (9×9).

 The game includes:
- 1 wooden game board with grooves between squares for wall placement
- 

Twenty walls (flat two-space-wide pieces which can be placed in the groove that runs between the spaces)


- 4 pawns (typically in different colors)

### Setup


At the beginning the board is empty. Choose and place your pawn in the center of the first line of your side of the board, your opponent takes another pawn and places it in the center of the first line of his side of the board (the one facing yours).



**2-Player Game:** 

Then take 10 fences each.



**4-Player Game:** 

When the game starts, the 4 pawns are placed in the center of each of the sides of the board and each player is given 5 fences.



### Turn Structure


Each player in turn, chooses to move his pawn or to put up one of his fences.

 

When he has run out of fences, the player must move his pawn.



### Pawn Movement


The pawns are moved one square at a time, horizontally or vertically, forwards or backwards, never diagonally.

 

The pawns must bypass the fences.



#### Jumping Rules


When two pawns face each other on neighboring squares which are not separated by a fence, the player whose turn it is can jump the opponent's pawn (and place himself behind him), thus advancing an extra square.



**Diagonal Jump (Edge Case):** 

If there is a fence behind the said pawn, or the square behind him is out of the board, the player can place his pawn to the left or the right of the other pawn.





Multiple pawns may not be jumped. Walls may not be jumped, including when moving laterally due to a pawn or wall being behind a jumped pawn.



**4-Player Restriction:** 

The rules are identical to those for two players, but it is forbidden to jump more than one pawn.



### Wall Placement Rules


The fences must be placed between 2 sets of 2 squares.

 Walls:
- Must span exactly two squares (horizontally or vertically)
- Cannot overlap or cross existing walls
- 

Once placed, cannot be moved or removed



**Critical Rule:** 

You are not allowed to lock up your opponents pawn, it must always be able to reach its goal by at least one square.



### Win Condition


The game ends as soon as one of the players move their pawn to one of the nine spaces on the opposite side of the board. The player that moves their pawn across the board wins Quoridor.

## Play Experience

### Initial Impressions vs. Depth


Our initial thoughts were that the game was quite easy to play, with our 5 year old probably being fine with it, and that we were going to get bored of it rather quickly. However, we persisted in playing a few more games and found that we both started reading the game in a more detailed way that required better tactics and planning of our moves. It went from being a straight forward game to a mental challenge in no time at all.



### The Central Tension


With each turn you have to decide whether to advance with your pawn, block the other player, or try to make your route easier to get to the opposite side first and win the game.



### Strategic Feel


Quoridor is a fast-paced, strategic maze game where players race to reach the opposite side of the board. Players can also place walls to block their opponent's path, creating an ever-shifting maze. This clever combination of movement and obstruction makes Quoridor a thrilling game of tactical thinking and quick adaptation.



### The Reversal Dynamic


The maze you're constructing to block your opponent can unexpectedly become part of the maze your opponent is building against you. I've played more than one game of Quoridor where I was halfway across the board only to be fenced off from the side of the board I was racing along. That forced me to backtrack and head for the lone escape route on the other side of the board.

## Strong Points

### Elegant Simplicity


"The rules are not only few and short, they are Elegant and Pure in conception."





Quoridor exemplifies one of my favorite things about a good abstract game: you can teach it without saying a word. In fact, the rule booklet that comes with my Gigamic Games version is two pages long. One of those pages is simply drawings to illustrate the text and are really all you need to understand how the game is played.



### Accessibility with Depth


"Odd combination of being playable by grade school kids against Mom or Dad (with both enjoying the contest), with perfect or master level play being unobtainable without playing more often than you are ever likely to --- simple enough for kids to play intelligently, yet no ceiling effect for a smart adult."



### Comebacks Possible


"A player who is losing can suddenly be winning, if play is reasonably clever."



### Quick Learning Curve


"By the second game, players begin to notice tactics and strategies. This is important to enjoyability, because nobody enjoys struggling at a game where they never feel like they have any idea what they are doing."



### Component Quality


"Quoridor does maintain the Gigamic standard of having a fantastic board and nice, high-quality pieces. Quick game, too, which I've both come to expect and appreciate."



### Award Recognition


Quoridor received the Mensa Mind Game award in 1997 and the Game Of The Year in the United States, France, Canada and Belgium.

## Weaknesses / Criticisms

### Anti-Climactic Endgame


"The game can quickly turn into a race. Once all of the fences are placed, all that is left is to see who can get to the other side faster. This isn't a huge drawback, since the strategy is mostly in placing the fences, but it is somewhat anti-climactic once all of the fences are placed to just alternate moving pawns back and forth."



### Determinism Once Walls Exhausted


"In one, we just kind of ended up building hallways and at the halfway point of the game I knew I had lost. I could no longer block my opponent and they just simply had to … get there. They had fewer steps to the end than I did, so, I resigned."



### Four-Player Issues


"I can see how it might appeal to players looking for a game where they're constantly guessing their opponents' moves and blocking them (especially at four players, where there will likely be a kingmaker in many games)."





Though it can be played with three players, doing so is not recommended, because the third player does not have a player on the opposite side and thus has an advantage.



### Scales Differently at 4 Players


"I rarely see abstract games scale well beyond the two-player version. While Quoridor doesn't do this nearly as well as Ingenious, the four-player game requires a slightly different mindset."

## Strategic Depth

### Key Strategic Concepts

#### Balance Movement vs. Wall Placement


"The most crucial decision in Quoridor is when to move versus when to place walls. New players often make one of two mistakes: Moving too much: Racing straight toward the goal without controlling the board. Walling too much: Focusing entirely on blocking the opponent while neglecting your own progress."



#### Dual-Purpose Walls (Offense + Defense)


"The first thing that I like about Quoridor is that a good player can use his fences for both offense and defense. One way is pretty obvious - put fences in front of your opponent. However, once you become good at the game, you will be able to use your fences to protect yourself as well."



#### Forcing Invalid Moves


"If you are smart with your fences, you are able to play your fences so that your opponent cannot play their fence as effectively as they would like. You do this by playing your fence so that if your opponent plays theirs in response it will completely block off the path."



#### Counting and Path Awareness


"Counting is important in Quoridor, especially midgame, after openings have been survived, players have moved some, and the shape of the final path is coming into definition. We have all had that close game. Walls are flying down, attacks, responses, and the two pawns quickly get locked into a shared path."



### What Separates Novices from Experts

#### Wall Conservation


"More experienced players of course know that this will leave the newer player without any walls left to play and no control over the board. In fact, the Quoridor veterans take this very strategy and turn it 180, saving their walls to push their own pawns towards the goal."



#### Board Edge Awareness


"Watch the edges of the board, and remember, there are four. It is not uncommon to find yourself pushed towards one side of the board while an open path still remains on the other side. While this path on the other side of the board remains open your opponent has the chance to completely block you off and send you back around the board. It is sometimes beneficial to spend a wall to close off a path far away from your pawn, especially if it leaves you only good options."



#### Opening Theory
Named openings exist, such as:
- 

"Ala opening: Both players advance their pawns three times (1.e2 e8 2.e3 e7 3.e4 e6), then the first player places one wall horizontally behind their pawn (d5h)."


- 

"The Rush is a good way to open with relatively safe aggression. It forces your opponent into an unwanted position. Their movement options are limited, and if they put a wall in the wrong place, it can be easily countered by movement or another wall placement."



#### Avoiding Bad Openings


"Sidestepping: Within the first three moves the first player moves their pawn horizontally left or right, giving the opponent the opportunity to move forward or place the first wall. The goal is to make a corridor to make the opponent go backwards. However, by doing so, the first player loses their spatial advantage and the second player can force the first player go backwards. Therefore, this is considered as a bad move."

## Implementation Notes

### Board Representation
The board is a 9×9 grid of cells (squares). Walls exist *between* cells, in grooves. A common approach:
- 

"The board is a plain Vec<Node>. A node represents the 'state' of a tile: right and down specify whether a player could move right/down from this tile. Having left and up is redundant, as two adjacent tiles would be holding 'duplicate' information."



Alternatively, walls can be represented by their position in an 8×8 grid of wall slots (since walls span 2 cells, there are 8 possible horizontal positions per row and 8 vertical positions per column).

### Wall Position Notation


"Every wall touches four squares. A wall move is denoted by the closest square to a1, with a horizontal h or vertical v orientation. For example, a vertical wall between columns e and f and spanning rows 3 and 4 is denoted e3v."



### Tricky Rules to Implement

#### 1. Path Validation for Wall Placement
The hardest rule to implement correctly is ensuring every wall placement leaves a valid path for all players to reach their goals.



"I am really happy that the game is fully playable for two with all the rules. Contrary to my previous challenge, I had to code my own pathfinding algorithm to detect whether putting up a fence is possible."



**Implementation approaches:**
- 

"One of the advantages of the graph datatype is that this datatype makes it easy to perform graph search. These search algorithms are used for two reasons. First, depth-first search is used to check whether there is [a valid path]."


- 

"Dijkstra's algorithm is ideal for use on a Quoridor graph, because it has optimal performance on sparse graphs."


- BFS also works well for validating path existence and finding shortest paths.
