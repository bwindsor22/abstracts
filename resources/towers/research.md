# Santorini Board Game: Complete Reference Guide

## Rules Summary

### Components


The game includes 22 Bottom Blocks, 18 Middle Blocks, 14 Top Blocks, 18 Domes, 6 Builders, 30 God Cards, 1 Play Board, 1 Decorative Cliff, and 1 Instruction Sheet.



### The Board


The game is played on a 5-by-5 grid of spaces. Each space in the grid has a level describing a tower that is up to 4 elements tall. Spaces all start out without any tower.

 

The tower on each space is represented as a level number from 0 to 4.



### Setup


The youngest player becomes the Start Player. The Start Player places two Workers of their color on any unoccupied spaces. The other player(s) place their Workers afterward. The Start Player takes the first turn.



### Turn Structure
Each turn consists of exactly two mandatory steps:

**Step 1: Move**


Move your selected Worker into one of the (up to) eight neighboring spaces. A Worker may move up a maximum of one level higher, move down any number of levels lower, or move along the same level. A Worker may not move up more than one level. The space your Worker moves into must be unoccupied (not containing a Worker or Dome).



**Step 2: Build**


Build a block or dome on an unoccupied space neighboring the moved Worker. You can build onto a level of any height, but you must choose the correct shape of block or dome for the level being built. A tower with 3 blocks and a dome is considered a "Complete Tower".



Key building rules:
- Level 0 → Place bottom block (largest)
- Level 1 → Place middle block
- Level 2 → Place top block (smallest)  
- Level 3 → Place dome (completes the tower)



A token can build on the space it just vacated in its move step.



### Win Conditions


You win immediately if one of your Workers moves onto Level 3.





If you cannot move and build on your turn, you lose.



Important clarification: 

To win the game by moving onto the third level, your Worker must move up during your turn. Therefore, if your Worker is Forced onto the third level, you do not win the game.



### Definitions (for precise implementation)
- 

Each space has up to eight spaces surrounding it. These are called its neighboring spaces.


- 

Unoccupied Space: A space not containing a Worker or dome.


- 

Perimeter Space: One of the 16 spaces along the edge of the board.


- 

Domes are not blocks. If the God Power description states it affects blocks, it does not affect domes.


- 

Some God Powers may cause Workers to be "forced" into another space. A Worker that is forced, is not considered to have moved.



### God Powers (Advanced Rules)


God powers are optional for 2 players but are mandatory for 3 or 4 players.



Setup with God Powers:


A player will need to be elected the challenger. This player will choose gods from the 30 available (while making sure they are compatible with the number of players). The challenger will then read from the rulebook each god power chose by name and ability. Clockwise from the challenger the players will choose a god power to use during the game.





Normal Rules and conditions still apply to you when using a God Power, with the exception of the specific changes described by the God Power. You must obey all God Power text that says you "cannot" or "must", otherwise you lose the game.

## Player Count & Duration



This game is for 2-4 players, ages 8 and up.





Santorini is best as a 2-player game. However, we have included rules for 3 and 4-players for those times when you really want to play with more people.



**Game Length:** 

In general most games should only take around 10-20 minutes.

 

Gameplay tends to be reasonably fast, and we finish the game in fifteen to twenty minutes. That said, like with any abstract strategy game, gameplay will ultimately depend on the players: this is the sort of game where you could ponder each move for several minutes.

## Play Experience

### Tension and Feel


The end result is a tense, quick, approachable game that rewards both long-term planning and short-term tactical flexibility.





Playing with another adult, this game can be gruelling, within a handful of turns your plan has already spiralled widely out of control as your opponent storms over and begins thwarting each attempt to build, or worse yet, races up your edifice that you have painstakingly built. Although there is no direct conflict, no workers are removed from the game and buildings are not torn down. Instead, the mere presence of the "enemy" is conflict enough.



### Strategic Nature


In one sense, Santorini is a race, an Olympic sprint to be the first player to reach the top floor of a three-story building. It's also a punishing game of spatial relationships, as each player uses the restrictions on both movement and building to stymie their opponent's plans.





Santorini does what all great abstract strategy games do, it forces you to plan way ahead, to anticipate your opponent. You're never just thinking about this turn, never simply reacting. Everything has a consequence, and due to the slick rules, these consequences are all visible, just not always seen.



### Perfect Information


All the information is perfectly visible to all players and so tracing the series of actions that lead to a win is easy. There are no complex choices, just a complex series of simple movements and buildings.





Luck plays no role in Santorini as players have full control over their moves and the game's outcome is determined by strategy and skill.

## Strong Points

### Elegant Simplicity


The simple rules unfold into deep strategies.





Under the hood, Santorini is a diamond-cut abstract design, a collection of no more than five or six rules that add up to a great game. Every turn is the same: move, then build. That's all you do.



### Production Quality


Every detail — from the eye-popping game board to the beautiful (and functional) tower pieces — is top notch. Santorini will always be an abstract game to me, but because of the production value and theming, I've managed to trick my abstract-averse friends into trying the game, and they've enjoyed it.





The greatest trick that Santorini ever pulled was making an abstract strategy game feel thematic. It is through the great use of components, cutesy art, and especially the overall physical experience of the game that this is possible.



### Variable Powers Add Replayability


The god cards turn a moderately interesting game into a masterpiece. The cards are well balanced and after revealing your cards each player will likely wish for the other player's power. The base game includes 30 god cards which allow for a dizzying array of combinations.





Santorini on the other hand really only has a handful of rules, and changing any one of these makes it a very different game. It still feels and plays the same, but the decisions you need to make become entirely coupled to the specific setup of the match. The god cards don't simply alter the efficiency of a fixed strategy – they create and reinvent and reinterpret strategies and force you to adapt on the fly.

## Weaknesses / Criticisms

### God Power Balance Issues


The God Powers cards can add a decent amount of luck to Santorini. Instead of a game that relies entirely on your strategy, the god matchup will have an impact on the outcome. This mostly comes from all of the gods not being created equal. Each god's power is interesting as it gives you something that can make it easier to win the game. Some gods are considerably more powerful than others though. Some god abilities will give you a slight edge while others can be gamebreaking.





It is not always that one god is better than another either. Some gods just have a huge advantage over other gods. In these cases their abilities mostly neutralize the other god's ability while still providing an additional benefit to their player. This adds a lot of luck to the game since if you get into a bad matchup you are going to have a hard time winning unless you have a considerably better strategy than your opponent.



### Skill Gap Problem


Like many games of this ilk, Chess being the obvious example, Accumulated Wisdom becomes a factor, as player refinement can mean that experienced Santorini players will find less enjoyment and challenge in games against someone less experienced. On the flip-side of that, the new gamer at the table probably won't enjoy being trounced.





Playing with God Powers is a great way to level the playing field, especially for kids or family members who aren't deep into strategy games. Without them, a more experienced player can steamroll their opponent round after round, which quickly drains the fun.



### Works Best at 2 Players Only


Santorini works best with 2 players, which may not be ideal for those who prefer games that can accommodate larger groups.





While functional, the three and four-player modes dilute the tight strategy that makes the game shine.



### Take-That Elements (with certain God Powers)


The game can suffer from a 'take that' quality if you are using the god powers, especially the advanced powers. What I mean by that is, each person gets to select a god power that can block or sometimes even remove a player's piece from the board. I had such a power and it did not make my daughter happy at all.

## Strategic Depth

### Key Concepts Beginners Miss

**1. Maintaining Height**


The biggest advice I can give is to not let yourself be in a position where you have to jump down. If you lose height in this game it can be extremely difficult to get it back.



**2. Using Both Workers Effectively**


Make sure you're taking advantage of having two workers, and always try to anticipate your opponent's next move.





If you can create a gap between the player's two pieces – and get both your pieces in between, your build options are vastly superior and they can almost certainly not out run you. Many early players will focus on building up with just one character and cutting them off with both of yours will likely trap them in a corner and leave you with 2-1 and the rest of the board to play.



**3. Blocking Strategies**


The first one is level-2 blocking, by building up the level-1 your opponent just built. In the mid-game, level-3 blocking is a common strategy too, by towering up every level-2 your opponent builds.



**4. Using Workers as Blockers**


Another key trap is to use your own builder to block. Sometimes it's worth keeping them on a lower level just to block that tile being accessible to your opponent. That might be just before building the third level (so your opponent cannot cap it), or it might be to stop your opponent being able to move before they can build.



**5. Corner and Perimeter Awareness**


Every inner space has 8 neighbouring spaces, but a perimeter space only has 5, and a corner space only has 3. That's why winning always occurs in perimeter space. Opponent has fewer options to block your winning in corner space (Only 3).



**6. The Corner-L Strategy**


If you have a worker on level-2 and you are in the corner, and there are two level-2's next to you, forming exactly a "L-shape" in the corner, in most of the cases, you win. For example, you are on level-2 on E5 and there are two level-2's on E4 and D5. This is because if the opponent worker moves to D4, you can build a third level on E5 and you win. If the opponent knows that and stays away however, you can move your second worker over to D4 and build at the corner. This makes you block that space and get the victory.



### What Separates Novice from Expert


Like the abstracts of old — Chess, Checkers, Go, etc. — Santorini demands planning several moves in advance. Even after dozens of plays I haven't figured out the best strategy.





There are in total 64 gods in BGA Santorini. However it does not mean there are only 64 strategies for god matchups. This is because every matchup is composed of two gods. Therefore you not only need a specific strategy on your gods, but also on how to use your god well to counter the opponent's god.

## Implementation Notes

### Core Data Structures Needed

1. **Board**: 5×5 grid where each cell stores:
   - Building level (0-4, where 4 = domed/complete)
   - Worker presence (none, player 1, player 2)

2. **Workers**: Each player has exactly 2 workers, track their positions

3. **Game State**: Current player, phase (setup/play), win/loss status

### Movement Validation Rules

A move from position A to position B is valid if and only if:
1. B is one of A's 8 neighbors (orthogonal or diagonal)
2. B is unoccupied (no worker, no dome)
3. `level(B) <= level(A) + 1` (can step up at most 1 level)
4. Note: Can step down any number of levels
